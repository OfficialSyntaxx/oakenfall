Chapter II — The Trades

Gatherers

Woodcutters, miners, foragers, hunters, and fishers keep raw goods flowing. Every gatherer works a real spot on the map and hauls the load home by hand.

Crafters

Sawyers, bakers, and other processors turn raw goods into planks, bread, and better things — if their building stands, has supply, and hasn't decayed.

Farmers

Fields follow the seasons: sow in spring, tend through summer, race the frost at harvest. A full granary is the difference between a hard winter and a funeral.

Guards

Knights and watchmen man the towers and palisades. Bandit raids probe your weakest wall — an unguarded hold is an invitation.

Chapter III — The River & The Roads

Bridges & fords

The river splits every hold. Two shallow rows let settlers wade across slowly with no bridge; build a bridge and they cross freely, opening the far bank's timber, stone, and hunting grounds.

The river as a moat

In defense the river counts as a natural moat — but every bridge erodes that bonus, so each crossing is a real trade-off. A guard post within three tiles keeps a bridge "watched" so it no longer weakens your walls, and raiders will name the unwatched span they slipped across.

Winter freeze

When winter bites the river freezes solid: anything can walk the ice — the moat bonus vanishes — and fishing halts until the thaw. Plan your defenses for the season the water turns to road.

Road logistics

Processors sitting beside a road network connected to the Town Center run about 12% faster. Paving isn't just for looks — a well-linked hold simply produces more.

Chapter IV — The Great Oak (Research)

Many branches

Research grows like the ancient oak the hold is named for: thirteen technologies and policies across two tiers, from sharpened tools to aqueducts and cold storage, each unlocking new buildings, bonuses, or ways to endure.

Hold tiers

As your settlement grows it rises through hold tiers — camp to village to true hold — opening the shop, daily bounties, and grander construction.

Chapter V — The Four Roads (Game Modes)

Settler

The gentle road. Forgiving pace, softer winters — learn the hold without burying half of it.

Peaceful

No raids, no wolves at the door. Just you, the land, and the seasons.

Iron Winter

The merciless road. Scarcer food, crueler cold, bolder bandits. For stewards with scars.

Merchant

Coin is king — trade-focused play built around the market, bounties, and the shop.

Chapter VI — Surviving Your First Winter

1. Wood before glory

Your first autumn is for firewood and food, not monuments. A cold villager is an unhappy one; a hungry one starts packing.

2. Repair before it rots

Buildings decay. Below 35 condition their bonuses stop entirely — patch roofs in autumn, not in a blizzard.

3. Watch morale

Morale is a slow tide. Taverns, full bellies, families, and safety raise it; illness, hunger, and raids drain it. Villagers who lose heart leave for good.

4. The hold works while you sleep

Close the game and your villagers keep at it at reduced pace. Come back to stocked shelves — within reason.

Chapter VII — Hardships of the Hold

Fire

Timber buildings can catch — worst in a dry summer. Flames spread between neighbours, so space your timber, pave stone breaks, and dig a Well nearby; tap a blaze to fling water, and settlers rush to help.

Blight

A sickness that strikes a crowd and spreads between those standing close. Herbal Lore softens it and a Healer clears the sick — keep folk from bunching when the fever comes.

Climate spells

Beyond the daily weather, droughts, cold snaps, and fair spells settle in for days at a time — cutting crops and raising fire risk, draining food and warmth, or blessing the fields. Watch the clock.

Livestock & guilds

A Pasture grazes a herd for hands-off food (but needs winter fodder), and two Masters of a trade found a guild that lifts that craft hold-wide. The deeper your hold, the more it rewards.
